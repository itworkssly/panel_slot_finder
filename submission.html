Panel Slot Finder

Scheduling a multi-interviewer panel takes a coordinator one to two hours of email back-and-forth and timezone math. I built the one piece that removes most of that: a tool that reads messy availability and reconciles it against who is trained, who has capacity, and when the candidate is free, in one pass.

Live prototype Single-file HTML / JS Parsing on the Claude API Built in ~2 hours

The problem

Where the time goes

The team runs an ATS with basic scheduling. It handles a single-interviewer screen fine and breaks the moment a panel needs three people in one block. Every panel routes through one coordinator, by hand, over email. At 25 open roles in a sprint, scheduling alone is what makes the sprint miss its deadline.

I read the problem as four failures that all share one root cause:

  • 01Availability arrives as free text in email and Slack, so a human reads and re-keys it every time.
  • 02Conflicts and double-books are caught late, because nobody checks every constraint at once.
  • 03Coverage gets cut under time pressure: a slot gets filled by whoever is free, trained or not.
  • 04A reschedule means starting the whole panel over from scratch.

Part 1 · Workflow map

The full flow, and the piece I built

From the moment a candidate is ready to schedule through to the confirmed panel written back to the ATS. Amber steps are manual today. The teal step is the one I built, because every other step depends on getting it right.

Late conflicts, reschedules, cut corners on coverage: they all trace back to one thing, reconciling by hand. Fix the reconcile and the steps around it get faster on their own.

Why the input does not need to be clean

People answer availability in plain language. So the tool reads plain language. A coordinator pastes whatever came back, in whatever shape it arrived, and the parse turns it into time blocks the scheduler can use. This is the reason AI sits on this step where a structured form would fail.

AI does the reading. Deterministic code does the scheduling math. Each tool does the job it is actually good at.

Part 2 · Prototype

What I built and how to run it

Open the live tool→

Two views. The scheduler reads pasted availability, reconciles it against the trained-interviewer pools and the candidate's hours, sequences the loop with buffers, and produces a confirmed panel plus the ATS write-back. The sprint dashboard sits on top of a sample of 25 active reqs and shows what the reconciliation captures across a whole sprint: coordinator hours saved, coverage held, and which interviewer pool is getting stretched thin.

What is real and what is simulated. The parse and the scheduling math are real. Demo mode runs on baked scenarios so the tool works without an API key. Live parse mode makes a real Claude API call against text you paste, using your own key. The calendar pull and the ATS write-back are simulated, the write-back shows the exact payload it would send. I built it this way so a reviewer can see it work without setting anything up.

The decision I am most willing to defend

I did not try to build a scheduler. The ATS already schedules. I built the reconciliation and coverage engine, the piece the ATS does badly, and I made one rule non-negotiable: the tool never fills an interview with someone untrained for it. When no trained person is free in the candidate's window, it routes the decision to train-or-override instead of faking coverage. That rule is the difference between a faster booking tool and a tool that protects interview quality under time pressure.

AI usage & build note

How I worked with AI on this

Which tools, and what I used them for

I worked in a structured Claude project I maintain for this kind of work, with a set of specialist review personas I have built up over time. I used the architect persona for code audits on the single-file app, and editor and hiring-manager lenses to catch where my own framing was overreaching. Claude wrote and revised the HTML and JS; I drove the decisions, ran the build, and tested it. The parsing step is built to run on the Claude API.

The most important decision: what I left out

I left out building a scheduler from scratch, full timezone support for every interviewer, a candidate-facing view, and drag-to-reorder. I named those as next steps rather than half-building them. The thing I kept was the reconciliation-plus-coverage core and the train-or-override rule. Scoping to the one high-leverage piece, and being able to say what I deliberately did not build, mattered more than feature count.

How I validated the AI's output

I did not trust the generated numbers on sight. Every dashboard figure is computed from the seeded sprint by the same logic the scheduler runs, so I could re-run it and check it held together. I ran the volume model against published recruiting-funnel benchmarks rather than guessing, and I pushed back when a metric did not measure what it claimed. The reschedule rate, for example, I dialed to a healthy 15% once I checked what healthy looks like.

One thing that did not work, and how I fixed it

My first dashboard measured time-to-schedule and modeled the sprint as 25 panels. Both were wrong. Time-to-schedule mostly measures how full calendars are, not anything the tool controls, so it overstated the tool's effect. And 25 panels badly undercounted the real volume of a 25-req sprint. I caught it, rebuilt the volume model from funnel benchmarks (about 75 panels, 225 interviews), and replaced the weak metric with coordinator hours saved and coverage, the two things the tool actually drives. Volunteering that weakness is, I think, a better signal than hiding it.

What I would do next with another hour

  • Full timezone support, with each interviewer's home zone pulled from their calendar.
  • A candidate-facing view so they can see their day and flag a conflict before the panel locks.
  • A live ATS write-back against a real Ashby sandbox, replacing the simulated payload.
